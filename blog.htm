Best Practices for Making Utility Purchases

1. Use software tools to determine the engineering criteria. 2. Write specifications in clear consise language that everyone understands. 3. Include all of your collegues and the operations department to review the specifications. Electric utilities buy a lot of material including breakers, switchgear, transformers, steel, foundations, etc. Westinghouse in the 70’s exemplified what many engineers use as their method of purchase today. They had specifications that itemize every feature available for a particular device. They had specifications that narrowed the search for particular classes of devices. They had services that would create turn- key systems. Everything was published. The only difference today is these are published online. Good engineers specialize in these purchases and reading these specifications. Really good engineers add system analysis to their toolbag. That is combining all of the parts in order to see the overall operations meets more general safety and performance specifications. There are many good general requirements such as providing low enough arc-flash in order for linepersons to work in confined spaces or maintaining voltage levels with a combination of capacitors and load tap changers (LTCs) in order to minimize LTC number of operations. Requirements such as insulation coordination or breaker coordination were common but long term projects, several months for one person, in the past. These studies would then be incorporated into the purchases. These new analysis could have similar time-frames without tools. We have two engineers and three technicians. Tying a resource up for that long prohibits use of these tools. This is why focusing on the individual requirements is more common. Software tools are absolutely necesary to perform the general studies. These include distribution system modeling, facility rating, and load analysis software. Training for these softwares will be critical and IT support will be absolutely critical in order to make it work. Most IT departments don’t have this type of background and it is a specialty just to get the software working. I didn’t say it would be easy. The point of a specification is not so that you can “hold the vendor’s feet to the fire.” I’ve seen many specifications repetitive and sometimes contradictory descriptions that either leave a vendor covering themselves by adding money to the bid or having won the bid so often that noone else dares to compete. The vendor and the engineer should be of one mind about what they are trying to provide. If you have taken the time to determine good requirements this will be easy to do. If on the other hand you have obtained specifcations from a particular vendor it will be difficult to know what is the important part of the specification. In that case you will essentially end up one vendor. When it comes to maintenance the operations department is king. Most of the time they get called out in the middle of the night and have a lot of pressure to “make it work.” They have half the user experience, the other half is the customer which has pretty well-defined criteria in the electric industry. There is nothing better to determine their requirements than to show up in the middle of the night along side of them. You can also observe construction.
